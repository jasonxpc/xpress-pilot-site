File Setup

Preparing your artwork file

  1. 1 Set document size to final trim size — do not include bleed in your document dimensions.
  2. 2 Add 0.125″ bleed on all sides. Extend backgrounds and images beyond the trim edge to avoid white borders.
  3. 3 Keep text and logos 0.125″ inside the trim edge (safe zone) to prevent accidental clipping.
  4. 4 Set color mode to CMYK — not RGB. Colors shift at print when converted from RGB, especially bright blues and greens.
  5. 5 Resolution: 300 DPI minimum. Screen images at 72 DPI will print blurry — always use high-resolution artwork.
  6. 6 Embed or outline all fonts to prevent text reflow or substitution errors on our end.
  7. 7 Save as PDF/X-1a or PDF/X-4 (preferred) or a high-res PDF. Avoid saving as Word, JPEG, or low-res PDF.
  8. 8 If unsure — upload what you have. We do a free file review on every order and catch issues before they cost you.

1. Bleed
When your design has color or images that go all the way to the edge of the page, you must extend that artwork 0.125" (⅛") beyond the trim edge. This is called bleed.

Why? Because printing presses cut stacks of paper, and small shifts happen. Without bleed, even a 1mm shift leaves a thin white sliver at the edge. Bleed eliminates that risk.
Standard bleed: 0.125" on all 4 sides

2. Safe Zone
The safe zone is the area 0.125" inside the trim edge where all critical content — text, phone numbers, logos, faces — must stay.

Anything outside the safe zone risks being cut off. Even if it looks fine in preview, the physical cut can shift slightly. Keep it inside.

3. Gutter
The gutter is the inner margin area near the spine of a bound document (booklets, brochures, perfect-bind books). When pages are folded and bound, the spine area pulls the inner edges inward — content too close to the center gets swallowed.

For saddle-stitch booklets, allow at least 0.25" of gutter margin. For thicker perfect-bound books, more is needed.
Booklets: min 0.25" gutter · Perfect bind: 0.375"+"

4. Resolution (DPI)
DPI = dots per inch. Screen images are 72 DPI — that's fine for screens but prints blurry and pixelated at full size.

All print files must be 300 DPI at final print size. If you scale a 72 DPI image up, increasing the DPI setting does not fix it — the pixel data is already degraded. Always start from a high-resolution source.
Minimum 300 DPI at final printed size

Set color mode to CMYK
Screens display color in RGB. Printers use CMYK — a completely different color system. If you send an RGB file, we convert it, but some colors shift noticeably — especially bright blues, greens, and neons.

In Illustrator: File → Document Color Mode → CMYK.
In Photoshop: Image → Mode → CMYK Color.
In InDesign: set color swatches to CMYK values.

Save as PDF/X-1a (best for offset print) or a high-resolution PDF with crops and bleeds checked.

In InDesign: File → Export → Adobe PDF (Print) → choose "PDF/X-1a" → Marks and Bleeds → check "Use Document Bleed Settings."

Set canvas to your final print size
Create a custom canvas at your trim size — the finished size in hand. Business card: 3.5 × 2". Flyer: 8.5 × 11". Postcard 5 × 7": set canvas to 5 × 7".

Do not add bleed to your canvas dimensions. Canva Pro adds bleed automatically on download.

Set canvas to final size + 0.25" on each dimension
Canva Free can't add bleed at export, so build it into your canvas. Add 0.25" to both width and height: a 5 × 7" postcard becomes 5.25 × 7.25". A 3.5 × 2" business card becomes 3.75 × 2.25".

This extra 0.125" around all four edges is your bleed — the file you download will already be the right size to submit.

What are Crop Marks? Small corner lines printed outside your design that tell the printer exactly where to cut. When you check "Crop marks and bleed" in Canva, these appear automatically — you don't draw them yourself. They're trimmed off and never appear in the final printed piece.
